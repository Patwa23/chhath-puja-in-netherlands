Chhath Puja 2025

A Festival of Gratitude to the Sun God (Surya Dev) and Chhathi Maiya

Festival Dates: October 25 to October 28, 2025

Chhath Puja is a four-day ancient Hindu festival primarily celebrated in Bihar, Jharkhand, and Uttar Pradesh.

About Chhath Puja

Chhath Puja is a major Vedic festival dedicated to the **Sun God, Surya**, and **Chhathi Maiya** (a form of Goddess Usha). Devotees, particularly women (known as 'Vrati'), observe a rigorous 36-hour fast (often *Nirjala* - without water) and perform rituals to thank the Sun for sustaining life on Earth and to pray for the well-being, prosperity, and longevity of their families and children. It is unique as it is one of the few Hindu festivals where the setting sun is worshipped along with the rising sun.

The Four Days of Chhath Puja 2025

Day 1: Nahay Khay (Saturday, October 25, 2025)

The festival begins with ritual bathing, usually in a holy river or pond, to purify the body and mind. Devotees then prepare and consume a single, pure **vegetarian meal**, typically including *Lauki Bhaat* (bottle gourd curry and rice), marking the last meal before the rigorous ritualistic fast begins.

Day 2: Kharna / Lohanda (Sunday, October 26, 2025)

Devotees observe a strict fast without water from sunrise to sunset. In the evening, after offering prayers, they break the fast by eating *Kheer* (sweet rice pudding made with jaggery) and *Roti* (flatbread) as **Prasad**. The challenging 36-hour *Nirjala Vrat* (fast without food or water) then commences.

Day 3: Sandhya Arghya (Monday, October 27, 2025)

This is the main day of the Puja. Devotees gather at the banks of a river or pond carrying elaborate offerings in bamboo baskets, including traditional sweets like **Thekua**, fruits, and sugarcane. They stand in the water and offer the first **Arghya** (offering of water and milk) to the **setting Sun** (Sandhya Arghya).

Day 4: Usha Arghya & Parana (Tuesday, October 28, 2025)

The final day involves going back to the water body before dawn to offer the second **Arghya** to the **rising Sun** (Usha Arghya). After this final offering and prayer, the devotees break their arduous 36-hour fast. This ritual is known as **Parana** or **Puran**, officially concluding the Chhath Mahaparv.
